Pulsar wind nebula: Evolution and Emission

Pulsars

Pulsars are magnetized fast rotating neutron star. They can be modelled as fast spinning magnetic dipole and thus emit radiation in pulses. The period of emission is their rotational period. A pulsar can be characterized by itâ€™s period \(P\) and its rate of change. The surface magnetic field can be approximated as \(B = 3.2 \times \sqrt{P \dot P}\) which typically ranges from \(10^{12} G\) to \(10^14 G\).

Pulsar Spin Down

Fast rotating magnetic dipole emits radiation and relativistic particles accelerate in the changing magnetic field. This produces a "wind" of magnetic field and relativistic particles. The energy dissipated in this process can be written as \(\dot E = 4 \pi^2 I \frac{\dot P}{P^3}\) where \(I\) is the moment of inertia.

Origin of Pulsar Wind (PW)

Pulsars originates in supernova explosions. After the explosion the rapidly rotating neutron star at the center of remnants produces a wind of high energy particles.

PW Termination Shock

By that time the rest of the supernova ejecta moves at a relatively slower speed with respect to this wind jet. The pulsar wind, thus, interacts with the SN ejecta and slows down rapidly. This is called a termination shock.

At the termination shock the ram pressure of fast moving jet is balanced by the internal pressure of the nebula. Using hydrodynamic equilibrium, we write \(R_t\), the radius at which this occurs as \(R_t = \sqrt{\frac{\dot E}{4 \pi c P}}\).

The pressure inside PWN is \(\frac{B^2}{8 \pi}\).

Evolution of PWN

Early phase

At early phase of SN, the SNR moves freely at a very high speed of about \(10^7 m s^{-1}\). The pulsar is at the center surrounded by a rapidly expanding PWN which is again surrounded by a rapidly expanding SNR. The energy emission remains relatively constant. The wind is over pressures and so expands supersonic.

Chevalie modelled this expansion as

\[R_{\mathrm{PWN}}=1.87\left(\frac{\dot{E}}{10^{38} \mathrm{erg} / \mathrm{s}}\right)^{0.254}\left(\frac{E_0}{10^{51} \mathrm{erg}}\right)^{0.246}\left(\frac{M_0}{\mathrm{M}_{\odot}}\right)^{-0.5}\left(\frac{t}{10^3 \mathrm{yr}}\right)^{1.254}(\mathrm{pc})\]

Mature PWN

generation of reverse shock

As the initial forward shock of PW reaches the termination shock, a reverse shock is created which moves in opposite directions of SNR and heats up the material. This phase is called Sedov-Taylor phase of SNe. As the SNR shell is expanding monotonically, the internal propagation of PW shock is adiabatic. McKee and Truelove shows that this phase is reached at:

\[t_{\mathrm{sr}}=0.209\left(\frac{E_0}{10^{51} \mathrm{erg}}\right)^{-0.5}\left(\frac{M_0}{\mathrm{M}_{\odot}}\right)^{\frac{5}{6}}\left(\frac{n_0}{\mathrm{~cm}^{-3}}\right)^{-\frac{1}{3}}\left(10^3 \mathrm{yr}\right)\]

Emission from PWN

Pulsar wind as goes into SNR materials, it form s a bubble that expands supersonically. The input power is generally of the form \[\dot{E}=\dot{E}_0\left(1+\frac{t}{\tau_0}\right)^{-\frac{(n+1)}{(n-1)}}\] where \(\tau_0 \equiv \frac{P_0}{(n-1) \dot{P}_0}\).

The emission can happen in three ways.

  1. synchrotron emission from PWM

  2. emission from shocked SNe

  3. emission from surrounding dust

Synchrotron emission

Synchrotron emission can be characterize by the power law \[Q\left(E_e, t\right)=Q_0(t)\left(E_e / E_0\right)^{-\gamma}\] and the spectrum can be found by integrating over \(E\). The total particle energy is \((1 + \sigma) \dot E(t)\).

emission from shocked SNe

As PW shock propagates through the ejecta material, it heats the medium up. The reverse shock balances the internal pressure and thus the heat cannot only expand the nebula. Instead the material, which is usually ionised emits thermally. For slow shocks this is in IR or optical range but X-ray emissions have also been observed.

emission from dust

Dust surrounding PWN that are produced by SN are heated by synchrotron radiation from PWN or by collision wit shocked gas. This emits blackbody radiation.

Fate of PWN

The SNR gradually dissipates over time. PWN can evolve to a bow-shock nebula surrounded by some slowly rotating or gaviational pulsars.
